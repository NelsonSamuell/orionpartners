Five red flags in procure-to-pay that most internal audits miss

Fraud & AuditJuly 2026·6 min read

Procurement fraud rarely announces itself. It hides in split purchase orders, recycled invoice numbers, and vendors who share directors with your own staff. A standard cyclical audit — sample the invoices, tick the approvals, move on — will pass most of it. Here is what a risk-based review of your procure-to-pay cycle should look for first.

1. Purchase orders split just under approval thresholds

If your policy routes anything above KES 500,000 to a second approver, look for clusters of orders at KES 450,000–499,000 to the same vendor in the same month. Splitting is the single most common way approval controls are defeated, and it is invisible unless you aggregate orders by vendor and period rather than reviewing them one at a time.

2. Recycled or near-sequential invoice numbers

A legitimate supplier invoices many customers; their invoice numbers should arrive at your accounts payable desk with large, irregular gaps. A vendor whose invoices to you run 101, 102, 103 is either brand new, invoicing only you — or was created to invoice only you. The same applies to invoice numbers that repeat with a suffix changed.

3. Vendors who share details with your own staff

Match the vendor master file against the payroll file: bank accounts, phone numbers, physical addresses, and — where registry data allows — directors. A vendor sharing a bank account or director with an employee in the procurement chain is a conflict at best and a shell at worst. This one test surfaces more confirmed fraud than any other single procedure we run.

4. Goods received that no one can locate

Three-way matching (order, receipt, invoice) only works if the receipt is real. Sample high-value deliveries and physically verify the asset or stock exists. Consumables, fuel, and services are the classic hiding places, because there is nothing left to count.

5. One person who never takes leave

The procurement officer who processes everything, never delegates, and hasn't taken annual leave in three years is not necessarily dedicated. Long-running schemes need continuous tending. Mandatory leave with a genuine handover is a fraud control, not an HR nicety.

Where to start

None of these tests require new software — they require the audit plan to rank the procure-to-pay cycle by fraud likelihood rather than by rotation schedule, and to run data-level analytics across the whole population instead of a pulled sample. That is what risk-based planning means in practice.
